Chebyshev's Theorem Formula

Chebyshev's Theorem has two formulas similar to each other. We will explain both of them here and break down the notation of it here. They are as under

  • P denotes the probability of an event.
  • X is a random variable that denotes the random experiment.
  • E(X) is the expected value of our event
  • σ² denotes the variance of our event
  • k is the boundary of the result, i.e., how far we want our result to be diverged from the expected.

Chebyshev's Theorem Proof

As per the Chebyshev's Theorem the probability that an observation will be more than k standard deviations from the mean is almost 1/k². For the Chebyshev's Theorem to be true you need to follow only 2 conditions i.e. underlying distribution has a mean and the other is average size of deviations away from the mean isn't infinite. To prove this inequality let us consider there exists a population of n values contain n1 values of x1 and n2 values of x2.

Let us assume the standard deviation of population to be σ, μ is the mean and k>1 is some positive real number.

σ2=(∑(xi−μ)2⋅ni)/n

Here the sum ranges over all distinct xi. Thus we get

σ2 > ∑(xi−μ)2⋅ni/n

Since the sum ranges only over those xi when (|xi−μ|≥kσ), we can express

σ2> ∑k2σ2⋅ni}{n} = k2σ2⋅∑ni/n = k2σ2⋅Pout,

where Pout is the proportion of the population outside k standard deviations of μ. Since we have

Pout< 1/k².

We can apply the inequality to any probability distribution in which both the mean and variance are defined. Thus we have

Pr(|X-μ| > kσ) < 1/k².

FAQs on Chebyshev's Theorem Calculator

1. What is Chebyshev's Theorem?

Chebyshev's Theorem also known as Chebyshev Inequality is a theorem in probability which describes the dispersion of data from its mean.

2. What is the importance of Chebyshev Inequalities?

Importance of Chebyshev's Inequalities is that they enable us to derive bounds on probabilities of mean, mean and variance of probability distribution are known.

3. What does Chebyshev's Theorem say?

Chebyshev's Theorem states that for any k>1, there exists atleast 1-1/k² of data lying within k standard deviations of the mean.
