What is the Empirical Rule?

The Empirical Rule is a statistical rule which expresses that, for normally distributed data, nearly all of the data will fall within three standard deviations likewise of the mean. Also, it is called the three-sigma rule or the 68-95-99.7 rule.

In a normally distributed data set, the distance from the mean in SD is the z-score. For example, a z-score of 2.0 is 2σ distance from the mean.

The Percentage Rules

The empirical rule is shattered into three percentages i.e., 68, 95, and 99.7. Therefore, the empirical rule is also known as 68 95 and 99.7 rule.

1. The initial part of the rule says:

68% of the data values in a normal, bell-shaped, distribution will lie within 1 standard deviation (within 1 sigma) of the mean.

2. The second part of the rule says:

95% of the data values in a normal, bell-shaped, distribution will lie within 2 standard deviations (within 2 sigmas) of the mean.

3. At last, the third part of the rules says:

99.7% of the data values in a normal, bell-shaped, distribution will lie within 3 standard deviations (within 3 sigmas) of the mean.

Empirical Rule Formula in Statistics

In statistics, the formula for the empirical rule is used to figure out the first, second, and third standard deviation, and also it anticipates the percentage chances of data falls under that deviation. The empirical rule formula can be mathematically expressed as,

  • First Standard deviation = µ - σ to µ + σ (68% data)
  • Second Standard deviation = µ - 2σ to µ + 2σ (95% data)
  • Third Standard deviation = µ - 3σ to µ + 3σ (99% data)

Where, µ = mean and σ = standard deviation

Step by Step Solution on How to Calculate the Empirical Rule with Example

For calculating the empirical rule, you just have to be provided with a mean and standard deviation for a normal distribution, bell-shaped. If not, you can also try using z-scores with the empirical rule. In this instance, the mean z-score is 0 and the SD is 1.

Nevertheless, mostly the statistics problems including the empirical rule will give a mean and standard deviation.

Let's consider that the bell-shaped, normal distribution has a mean μ of 50, and a standard deviation, σ of 5. Now apply the three-sigma rule (which is the empirical rule) and find the exact ranges.

  • Applying the empirical rule is adding and subtracting up to three SD's from the mean.
  • FIRST PART: First, subtract and add 1 standard deviation from/to the mean:

50 – 5 = 45

50 + 5 = 55

  • Hence, 68% of the values fall between scores of 45 to 55.
  • SECOND PART: Then, subtract and add 2 standard deviations from/to the mean:

50 – (2)(5)

50 – 10 = 40

50 + (2)(5)

50 + 10 = 60

  • Hence, 95% of the values fall between scores of 40 to 60.
  • THIRD PART: Lastly, subtract and add 3 standard deviations from/to the mean:

50 – (3)(5)

50 – 15 = 35

50 + (3)(5)

50 + 15 = 65

  • Hence, 99.7% of the values fall between scores of 35 to 65.
  • Thus, you will get the correct ranges.
